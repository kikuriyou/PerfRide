# Dr. Andrew Coggan's Power Training Zones for Cycling

Based on the foundational principles detailed in *Training and Racing with a Power Meter, 3rd Edition* by Hunter Allen, Dr. Andrew Coggan, and Stephen McGregor, the 7-zone power model remains the gold standard for structuring and analyzing cycling training.

## 1. The 7-Zone Power Table

| Zone | Name | % of FTP | Typical Duration | Primary Physiological Adaptations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | Active Recovery | < 55% | > 2 hours | Speeds recovery, clears metabolic byproducts, promotes blood flow to muscles. |
| **2** | Endurance | 56% - 75% | 2.5 - 14 hours | Increases mitochondrial density, capillary density, and fat oxidation; builds aerobic base. |
| **3** | Tempo | 76% - 90% | 1 - 3 hours | Improves muscular endurance, aerobic capacity, and glycogen storage capacity. |
| **4** | Lactate Threshold | 91% - 105% | 10 - 60 minutes | Raises FTP, increases fractional utilization of VO2 max, and improves lactate clearance. |
| **5** | VO2 Max | 106% - 120% | 3 - 8 minutes | Increases maximal oxygen uptake (VO2 max), cardiac output, and blood plasma volume. |
| **6** | Anaerobic Capacity | 121% - 150% | 30 sec - 3 minutes | Enhances anaerobic capacity (FRC), lactate tolerance, and muscle buffering capacity. |
| **7** | Neuromuscular Power | > 150% | < 30 seconds | Increases maximal sprint power, neuromuscular recruitment, and ATP-PCr stores. |

## 2. Heart Rate and RPE Correlation

While power is an objective measure of work, Heart Rate (% of Maximum Heart Rate) and Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE on a 0-10 scale) provide essential subjective and physiological feedback.

| Power Zone | % of Max HR | RPE (0-10) | Feeling & Breathing Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Z1: Active Recovery** | < 60% | < 2 | Extremely light effort. Conversation is effortless. |
| **Z2: Endurance** | 60% - 70% | 2 - 3 | Light effort. Can maintain full, continuous conversations easily. |
| **Z3: Tempo** | 70% - 80% | 3 - 4 | Moderate effort. Requires focus; conversation is limited to short sentences. |
| **Z4: Threshold** | 80% - 90% | 4 - 5 | Hard effort. Deep, rhythmic breathing; speaking is very difficult. |
| **Z5: VO2 Max** | 90% - 100% | 6 - 7 | Very hard effort. Ragged breathing; speaking is limited to single words. |
| **Z6: Anaerobic** | N/A (Lag time) | 8 - 9 | Extremely hard. Severe muscular burn; no speaking is possible. |
| **Z7: Neuromuscular** | N/A (Too short)| 10 | Maximal, all-out sprint effort. Total exertion. |

*(Note: Heart rate response lags significantly behind changes in power. Therefore, HR is not a reliable metric for pacing short, intense efforts in Zones 6 and 7).*

## 3. Training Distribution Models

How time is distributed across these zones over a macrocycle defines your training architecture:

### Polarized Model (80/20)
*   **Distribution:** Approximately 80% of sessions (or time) spent in low intensity (Z1/Z2) and 20% in high intensity (Z5+). Time in Z3 and Z4 is intentionally minimized ("the black hole").
*   **Philosophy:** Maximizes aerobic adaptations and cellular durability without accumulating central fatigue, leaving the athlete perfectly fresh for extremely high-quality, hard interval sessions.
*   **Best For:** Long-distance events, elite athletes with high training volumes (>12 hours/week), and avoiding overtraining.

### Pyramidal Model
*   **Distribution:** A large base of Z1/Z2, a moderate amount of Z3/Z4, and a small peak of Z5+ (e.g., 70% Z1/Z2, 20% Z3/Z4, 10% Z5+).
*   **Philosophy:** A smooth, gradual progression of intensity. It is less dogmatic than polarized training, allowing for crucial, event-specific threshold work.
*   **Best For:** Most amateur cyclists, traditional base-build-peak periodization, and moderately long events.

### Threshold / Sweet Spot Model
*   **Distribution:** Heavy emphasis on the upper end of Z3 ("Sweet Spot" at 88-93% FTP) and Z4, with the remainder filled by Z1/Z2.
*   **Philosophy:** Aims to maximize training stress and fitness adaptations in the shortest amount of time by targeting the physiological sweet spot between intensity and recovery.
*   **Best For:** Time-crunched athletes (6-8 hours/week), time trialists, and criterium racers.

## 4. Zone Adjustment Guidance When FTP Changes

Functional Threshold Power is a dynamic metric. As fitness shifts, power zones must be strictly recalibrated to ensure you are targeting the correct physiological energy systems.

*   **Testing Frequency:** Re-assess FTP every 4 to 6 weeks during build phases, or after major training blocks, using a validated protocol (e.g., standard 20-minute test, 8-minute test, or Ramp Test).
*   **Immediate Recalibration:** Upon establishing a new FTP (whether an increase or a decrease), mathematically recalculate all 7 zones immediately. A mere 10-watt change in FTP drastically alters the pacing required for Z4 and Z5 intervals.
*   **Adjusting Time-in-Zone (Progression):** When training with a newly elevated FTP, do not attempt your previous maximum time-in-zone immediately. For example, if you successfully completed 4x10 minutes at Z4 with your old FTP, start your new block at 3x10 or 4x8 minutes at the newly established power target, progressively building duration back up.
*   **The RPE/HR Sanity Check:** During your first few workouts with a new FTP, use your HR and RPE scales to validate the zones. If a Z4 Threshold workout pushes your HR into Z5 or feels like an RPE of 7, your new FTP may be artificially inflated (common with Ramp Tests). Conversely, if Z4 feels like a moderate Z3 effort, your FTP may be underestimated.
