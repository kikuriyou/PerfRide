# Cycling Training Periodization Guide

This document outlines the principles of training periodization for cyclists, based on the methodologies presented in *The Cyclist's Training Bible, 5th Edition* by Joe Friel.

## 1. Annual Training Phases and CTL Targets

A standard annual training plan (ATP) is divided into distinct periods, each with specific physiological goals and Chronic Training Load (CTL) targets.

### Preparation Phase (3-4 weeks)
*   **Focus:** Cross-training, gym work, light endurance. Rebuilding consistency after the off-season.
*   **Intensity:** Low (Zone 1-2).
*   **Weekly CTL Target:** Steady but low, gradually increasing from baseline (e.g., 30-40).

### Base Phase (8-12 weeks)
Divided into Base 1, 2, and 3.
*   **Focus:** Aerobic endurance, muscular endurance (force), and speed skills.
*   **Intensity:** Primarily Zone 2, introducing Tempo (Zone 3) and Sweet Spot later in the phase.
*   **Weekly CTL Target:** Steady, consistent growth. Aim for the highest volume of the year by Base 3. CTL might reach 60-80 depending on the athlete's level.

### Build Phase (6-8 weeks)
Divided into Build 1 and 2.
*   **Focus:** Race-specific fitness. Muscular endurance, anaerobic endurance, and sprint power. Volume decreases slightly while intensity increases.
*   **Intensity:** Threshold (Zone 4), VO2 Max (Zone 5), and Anaerobic Capacity (Zone 6) intervals.
*   **Weekly CTL Target:** Plateauing or very slow growth. The focus shifts from accumulating CTL to accumulating Acute Training Load (ATL) stress through high intensity. Peak CTL is usually achieved by the end of Build 2.

### Peak Phase (1-2 weeks)
*   **Focus:** Shedding fatigue (reducing ATL) while maintaining fitness (CTL) to achieve positive Training Stress Balance (TSB) or form.
*   **Intensity:** Very high (race pace), but volume drops significantly.
*   **Weekly CTL Target:** CTL will begin to slowly decline as volume drops.

### Race Phase (1-3 weeks)
*   **Focus:** Competing at peak performance.
*   **Intensity:** Race efforts, with active recovery in between.
*   **Weekly CTL Target:** CTL will continue to slowly decay. TSB should be highly positive (+15 to +25) for A-priority races.

### Transition Phase (2-6 weeks)
*   **Focus:** Physical and mental recovery.
*   **Intensity:** Very low, unstructured riding.
*   **Weekly CTL Target:** CTL drops significantly (often back down to 30-50).

## 2. Loading Patterns (Macrocycles)

Structuring training blocks involves balancing stress (overload) and recovery.

### The 3:1 Loading Pattern
*   **Structure:** Three weeks of increasing training load (duration and/or intensity) followed by one week of recovery (reduced volume, maintained frequency, perhaps reduced intensity).
*   **Best For:** Younger athletes, those with lower overall training stress, athletes in the Base phase, and riders with high recovery capacity.

### The 2:1 Loading Pattern
*   **Structure:** Two weeks of increasing training load followed by one week of recovery.
*   **Best For:** Older athletes (Masters, typically 40+), athletes with high life stress, athletes in the high-intensity Build phase, or those prone to overtraining.

## 3. Goal-Specific Periodization

The emphasis during the Build phase changes dramatically based on the athlete's primary event.

### Hillclimb / Time Trial (TT)
*   **Key Limiters:** Sustained power at lactate threshold (FTP), power-to-weight ratio.
*   **Build Phase Focus:** Extensive Muscular Endurance. Long intervals at or just below FTP (Zone 4 / Sweet Spot). E.g., 2x20 mins, 3x15 mins. Focus is on extending the duration the athlete can hold target power.
*   **Peak Phase:** Maintaining FTP while shedding fatigue.

### Road Race / Criterium
*   **Key Limiters:** Anaerobic endurance, repeatability, sprint power, VO2 max.
*   **Build Phase Focus:** Intensive intervals. Repeated surges above threshold, VO2 max intervals (Zone 5), and micro-bursts to simulate attacks and bridging gaps. E.g., 5x3 mins at VO2 Max, 30/30s.
*   **Peak Phase:** Sharpening the "matchbook"—ensuring maximal freshness for repeated high-power efforts.

### General Fitness / FTP Increase
*   **Key Limiters:** Aerobic engine capacity, sustained power.
*   **Build Phase Focus:** A balanced approach. Progression from extensive Sweet Spot/Threshold work into targeted VO2 Max blocks to pull the "ceiling" higher, allowing room for further FTP growth.

## 4. Sequencing Constraints

Properly ordering workouts within a microcycle (week) and mesocycle (block) is critical to maximize adaptation and avoid overtraining.

*   **Hard vs. Easy Days:** Follow high-intensity or very long duration days with active recovery or complete rest days. Never sequence three high-intensity days in a row.
*   **Energy System Targeting:**
    *   Do not stack exhaustive anaerobic capacity (Zone 6) workouts on consecutive days.
    *   VO2 Max workouts (Zone 5) are best performed when fresh (e.g., after a rest day).
    *   Endurance (Zone 2) can be performed the day after a hard session.
*   **Strength Training:** In the Base phase, schedule gym sessions on the same day as cycling (ideally gym first, or hard cycling first followed by gym) to ensure true rest days remain restful. In Build/Race phases, gym frequency decreases to maintenance levels.

## 5. Taper Protocols

The taper (occurring during the Peak phase) reduces fatigue to reveal fitness.

*   **Duration:** Typically 7 to 14 days, depending on the severity of the preceding training block and the athlete's recovery profile.
*   **Volume Reduction:** Reduce training volume (duration) by 40-60% compared to the peak weeks of the Build phase.
*   **Frequency:** Maintain training frequency (days per week). Do not add extra rest days; keep the body accustomed to its routine.
*   **Intensity:** *Maintain high intensity.* Include short, race-pace efforts to keep the neuro-muscular system primed, but keep the total time-in-zone very low to prevent acute fatigue accumulation.
*   **TSB Goal:** Aim for a Training Stress Balance (TSB) of +15 to +25 on the day of an "A" priority race.

## 6. CTL Ramp Rates by Level

The rate at which CTL increases (Ramp Rate) dictates adaptation. Too slow, and fitness stagnates; too fast, and the athlete risks overtraining or injury. Ramp rate is measured in CTL points gained per week.

*   **Beginner / Low Volume:** 2 to 4 CTL points per week.
*   **Intermediate / Medium Volume:** 4 to 6 CTL points per week.
*   **Advanced / High Volume:** 6 to 8 CTL points per week (sustainable only for short blocks).
*   **Elite / Pro:** May sustain 7-9+ for short periods, but generally rely on massive baseline volume.

*Note: Ramp rates exceeding 7-8 points per week for sustained periods (more than 2-3 weeks) often lead to excessive fatigue (negative TSB dropping below -30), increasing the risk of illness and overtraining.*
